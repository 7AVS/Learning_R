Reading

SFMC/7020 is small and effectively 1012-only (≈16/mo, consistent with the separately-documented Exact Target trickle). Open question this does NOT yet answer

What this does NOT showDirect proof of human authorship — the table records which system wrote the row, not that a person, rather than an automated process behind that system, made the decision. "Assisted channel" is the recorded fact; "human-authored" is an inference from it. It also does not tell us anything about the writer mix specifically among the small bridged-flip subset (open question above), and it does not explain what 7033/7053 actually are.

Arithmetic reconciliation (the 1014 misread)

The 1014 cpc_flips figure was initially photo-misread as 3,642. It was corrected to 8,642 using two independent checks that both had to agree:

  • Cross-check against Z2 (Section 1): Z2's independently-built 13-month total for 1014 is 8,642 — an exact match to the corrected figure, not the misread one.
  • Internal arithmetic: cpc_flips must equal no_prior_unsub_found + bridged. With bridged = 100 (legibly read) and no_prior_unsub_found = 8,542 (legibly read), the total must be 8,642 — 8,542 + 100 = 8,642 ✓. The misread total (3,642) would have required no_prior_unsub_found to be 3,542, which is not what the photo shows.

The 1002 and 1012 rows needed no correction — they check out on both fronts already: 2,158 + 10 = 2,168 ✓ (matches Z2's 1002 total exactly); 1,511 + 30 = 1,541 ✓ (matches Z2's 1012 total exactly).

The "no cadence" reading

Where a CPC flip does bridge back to a prior vendor unsub, the gap between the two events is smeared across a wide range (average roughly 52–101 days across the three switches) with no visible clustering at a fixed interval that would suggest a periodic batch job (e.g., a consistent 7-day or 30-day lag). This is the timing-side half of the "no hidden sync" conclusion, independent of Z3's writer-side evidence.

What this showsThe overwhelming majority of CPC flips (98–99.5% depending on switch) have no vendor unsub anywhere in the prior 12 months — most flips are not preceded by an unsub at all. Of the small minority that are, the timing gap doesn't cluster at any regular interval, arguing against a periodic sync/middleman process.
What this does NOT showNothing about whether suppression/enforcement actually works — i.e., whether flipping a switch to "No" stops email from going out. That is a completely separate question (the 1014 dictionary-vs-lore enforcement question, covered in Section 6) and B-reverse says nothing about it either way. The bridged-row averages are also built on very small denominators (10 / 30 / 100 clients) — treat the "52–101 day" figure as a rough band, not a precise estimate.
Trust levelVerified-by-cross-check, after correction. Three scrolled photos of the same table exist (built-in redundancy), yet one figure was still misread on first pass — a reminder that having multiple angle-shots of a table does not by itself guarantee a correct read; the correction here relied on an independent arithmetic tie-out, not just re-looking at the photos.

The leak hint, and the DM-control confound

Baseline receive rate (no switches out) is ≈48.8% (E1) / 57.6% (E2's 0-0-0-0 row — different denominator, both from the same pack). Nearly all No-switches in E1 cluster in a ≈19–30% band regardless of whether the switch is plausibly email-relevant at all: 1007 (mail) sits at 19.5%, 1012 (the actual email switch) sits at 30.1% — a channel-specific switch and an obviously irrelevant one land in nearly the same band. That similarity is the "leak hint": clients who marked themselves "No" on switches that shouldn't touch email are still receiving email in-window at rates not far off from the switches that should. The repeated ~46–50K holder counts recurring across many unrelated switches point to the same bundled opt-out population (per the separately-documented bundle/selection finding) showing up again and again, rather than each switch individually gating email.

1007 was intended as a negative control (direct mail, not email — should show no relationship to email receipt), but E2 crosses it with the other three flags rather than isolating it, so its role as a clean control is muddied in this particular table.

What this showsA raw association: clients flagged "No" on almost any switch — email-relevant or not — receive email in-window at broadly similar, reduced rates relative to baseline, consistent with a bundle/selection confound (the same clients hit many switches at once) rather than each switch cleanly and individually gating email delivery.
What this does NOT showNo enforcement conclusion should be drawn from E1/E2 alone. Neither screenshot shows the underlying SQL or the date window — the window is unconfirmed (likely Q2-2026 per the file's design intent, but not verified). This is a raw cross-tab, not a matched-control comparison; it does not settle whether any specific switch actually stops email.

Reproduces pack-12 E1, independently

The main-cut rates (30.2% / 26.2% / 19.2% for 1012/1014/1002) match the recovered pack-12 E1 rates (Section 6: 30.1% / 26.3% / 19.2%) almost exactly, despite the two runs using different windows — E1's window is unconfirmed (likely Q2-2026) while 22-B anchors state to a confirmed 2026-04-01 date and measures receipt over the following quarter. Two differently-built reads landing on the same numbers is a real cross-check, not a rounding coincidence.

Removing the confound makes the leak worse, not better

The exclusivity cut isolates clients who hold only the named flag (no other gate flag set) — the cleanest available cut against the bundle/selection confound flagged in Section 6. If 1012 (the email-specific switch) actually gated email, the "only_this_flag" cut should show a markedly lower rate than the bundled population. It does not: 1012-only clients receive email at 47.2% — higher than the 1012 multi-flag cut (18.9%) and not far below the 57.9% unflagged baseline. Stripping out the confound did not reveal a hidden enforcement effect; it removed the one thing that had been holding the rate down.

Construction note and residual caveat

The vendor feed used for "received_email_in_window" is built from NBA campaign TREATMENT_IDs — these are marketing sends by construction, so "this is all service mail, not marketing" is not an available explanation for the observed rates. The narrower residual caveat: a minority of MNEs in the tracked universe may carry non-promotional content (see the MNE dictionary, knowledge doc §4); which specific MNEs reach flagged clients is a follow-up, not yet run.

What this showsClients who explicitly opted out on 1002, 1012, or 1014 as of 2026-04-01 nonetheless received campaign email in the following quarter at rates (19–30% in the main cut, up to 47% in the 1012-only exclusivity cut) not far below the unflagged baseline (57.9%). The gap does not close when the bundle confound is removed — for 1012 it widens.
What this does NOT showA confirmed root cause. This is a state-vs-outcome cross-tab, not a controlled experiment, so it cannot distinguish "targeting logic never checks CPC at send time" from "CPC→SFMC suppression sync is partial/broken" — both are consistent with the same numbers.
